# Over optimization, under optimization, intuition

I want to try and explain some of my intuition for the
recent optimization I did.

I do not know whether my change is beneficial or not.

I need to get better at measuring things.
I tend to avoid measuring things when working on my own projects
and just go by the "feel" of things.
This is because measuring is extremely difficult and often misleading.
If I were making a change for someone else's code I'd
want to have something more than just a "feeling".
But nevertheless... I want to think about my decision.

- Garbage collection red flags
- Incremental optimization
- Localized overhead vs appwide
- Do I believe that I have written the localized optimization correctly

Understanding at least the basics of garbage collection is extremely important.

I'd go as far as to say if you don't understand the basics,
your likelihood of "poisoning" a codebase is extremely high and everything you do ought to be quarantined.

This isn't to say that I don't bad code.
But when I write bad code I usually do so with an understanding of the goal,
and an understanding of whether I'm creating problems in the app as a whole.

- Garbage collection red flags:

with respect to the previously described code, the a major GC red flag
is the creation of all the individual span elements whenever you draw a line.

I would probably agree with you if you brought up the innerText calculations being an even bigger red flag.
But in terms of complexity, and reasonably being able to optimize something.
The spans stand out as a red flag AND they have a optimization that sounds quite reasonable.

i.e.: when drawing the text and re-using previously drawn lines of text.
You might be able to reduce the GC overhead of scrolling by re-using not only the existing divs,
but also the existing spans.

So, the change was to check if the previously drawn line of text
has an already instantiated span element that is free to have its
innerText swapped out.
- if so, then do exactly that
- otherwise you need to construct a new span element

Then, when you're done drawing the line,
if the previously drawn line of text
had more spans than what you needed,
you remove each of those extra spans.

- Incremental optimization:

I wanted to "feel" out what doing the previously described optimization would be like.
So I did the mosts straightforward implementation of such an optimization.
I'm not sure if I like my implementation, but regardless it brings me closer
to feeling out whether it is a good change for the app as a whole.
And then I can continually revisit the implementation and refine it over time
if I feel it is of use.

But, I haven't actually measured anything.
This would be far more problematic if my implementation
added "app-wide" overhead.
But the implementation is localized, and thus I'm not overly bothered by this.

*"app-wide" overhead is quite a hand wavy statement I'd have to determine my exact definition at a later point.
... because all code in the short term is probably causing some degree of overhead.
... I suppose I more so refer to whether the object allocations necessary would be long living or short living.

When it comes to incremental optimization though,
the final step is to remove all the extra spans that I didn't re-use (if any exist).

This throwing away of the extra spans could be "optimized" such that you have a cache somewhere that you can store these spans.
Then if you hit a new line of text that needs more spans than what the previously drawn line of text has.
You can pull from this cache to get a span instead of allocating a new one.

- Localized overhead vs appwide:

But, this leaps from localized overhead to app-wide overhead.
And for a hand wavy optimization such as this where I have no measurements I just kind of want
to feel things out.
To cause app-wide overhead for a "maybe optimization" is extremely foolish.

- Do I believe that I have written the localized optimization correctly:

I'm actually completely unsure.
My goal was to put the pieces in place in a way that reasonable.
So that the groundwork for the optimization exists and then you can paste over the existing paragraphs of code
that perform the current implementation, to whatever the end result implementation might look like.

I'm not even entirely adverse to the idea that my current implementation
might be slower than no optimization at all.

But, it gives me that starting point and easy to change details.
Otherwise procrastination and analysis paralysis will have you
wondering whether you should do the optimization for your entire life
and you never actually get around to writing a single line of code for it.

A "hack" is a negative description.
"Hacking" something together is too I believe has a negative connotation.

But a good programmer can write little snippets of "hacked together code"
in a way that is organized.

And then the final separator is that
a "hack" believes the "hacked together code" is the final result.

A good programmer acknowledges that all code is flawed
and thus a tiny bit of organized "hacking" to feel something out
can be extremely useful, and that your "hacked together code" isn't the final result,
but instead a series of steps to get the true best solution
because you can feel out the requirements of the app.

I am a bit sloppier than what I'd like.
It depends, sometimes I am just exhausted.
And I understand the cost to benefit ratio of things.
So due to being exhausted the code I write that day is extra sloppy.
Is it worth while to write that snippet of code,
or should I avoid putting that hard to read code in the app.
Deadlines, emotional states,
the fact that progress improves emotional state,
the fact that those hard to read code snippets can very well destroy your app in the long run,
it is all cost to benefit analysis moment to moment.

But, since I'm the only developer on this repo
I am more open to being a bit sloppier
because the emotional state of doing something like this
is extremely difficult.
And I need to make sure I don't abandon the project due to seeking perfection in every line of code.
Meanwhile I'm the only one writing that code so the perfection slows everything to a halt,
and the stress is high,
and you abandon the project.

I think deeply about my good code.
And I think deeply about my bad code.
They are all a means to an end that I have mapped out years ahead of time.
